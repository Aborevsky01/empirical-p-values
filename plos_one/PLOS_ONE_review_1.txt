Reviewer #1: I found this manuscript interesting, timely, and potentially valuable. The paper addresses an important practical problem, namely the reliability of false discovery rate control in classification settings affected by distribution shift, which is highly relevant in modern machine learning applications, especially in biomedical contexts. The proposed Test Null Adjustment (TNA) approach is simple, intuitive, and appealing because it is data-driven, model-agnostic, and operates directly in the one-dimensional prediction score space rather than in the original high-dimensional feature space.

A major strength of the paper is that the authors clearly motivate why distribution shift can invalidate empirical p-values and compromise downstream FDR control, and they present a practical solution that does not require domain-specific assumptions. The manuscript is generally well structured, and the methodology is described in a way that makes the main idea easy to follow. I also appreciated that the authors evaluate the method across several biomedical datasets and under both data-distribution and class-distribution shift scenarios, which gives the study broader practical relevance. The comparative analyses with BH and LTT are useful, and the visual diagnostics based on Q-Q plots and FDP/FDR comparisons help the reader understand the behavior of the proposed method.

Another positive aspect is the balanced tone of the manuscript. The authors do not overstate the method and explicitly acknowledge that TNA is heuristic and does not yet come with a theoretical guarantee. This honesty strengthens the paper. The conclusion is also well aligned with the experimental evidence: the method appears especially useful when moderate shift affects calibration and standard EPV-based FDR control becomes unreliable, while not substantially harming performance when shift is absent.

Overall, I believe this is a worthwhile contribution. The paper tackles a real and understudied issue, proposes a method that is practically implementable, and supports it with several illustrative experiments. I think the manuscript is suitable for publication after minor revision. My only suggestions would be to further improve clarity in a few methodological details, such as the assumptions underlying TNA-/TNA+, the sensitivity to histogram binning choices, and the interpretation of the weaker performance on the BCSS dataset. These are relatively minor points and do not detract from the overall merit of the work.

Reviewer #2: This work introduces the Test Null Adjustment (TNA) method, a heuristic approach designed to ensure reliable False Discovery Rate (FDR) control in machine learning classifiers operating under data or class distribution shifts. The research outlines experiments using the Benjamini-Hochberg (BH) procedure and unidimensional scores across multiple datasets (MNIST, PCam, CheXpert, TissueNet, and BCSS).

The manuscript shows some methodological vulnerabilities, including the absence of theoretical validation that formally guarantees the error bounds of FDR estimates across different degrees of distributional shift, and the reliance on manual parameterization of histogram intervals. Also, while the mathematical framework is transparent, the manuscript fails to achieve 100% reproducibility due to the omission of environmental specifications and an accessible open-source repository containing the specific implementation code.
